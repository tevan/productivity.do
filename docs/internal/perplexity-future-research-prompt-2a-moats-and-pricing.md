# Part 2a of 7 — Data moats and pricing models

I'm a solo founder building a productivity surface (calendar + tasks + notes + projects + files). My current product: Svelte SPA + Express backend, Google Calendar sync, Todoist mirroring, markdown notes, lightweight projects (with due date, intent line, working-hour rhythm, momentum signal, pin-to-decision-surface), voice capture that auto-routes input to task/event/note/comment via Claude Haiku, MCP server for external agents. The differentiation thesis is "be the place where someone or their agent makes the decision about what to do next."

This is part 2a of an 8-part research pass (parts 4 and 5 already complete). Stay focused on this part only — I'm running the others as separate sessions.

Skip marketing fluff. Cite primary sources where possible. Tell me what I'm wrong about.

## What I want you to research

### 1. Data moats — real vs. apocryphal

If transcription, summarization, classification, and the model layer are all commodity, what's actually defensible in productivity? Cite specific evidence:

- **Cross-reference moats** — your Notion is connected to your Slack is connected to your Drive is connected to your calendar; switching means rebuilding all of it. Is this real, or do users actually switch frequently? What does churn data say at Notion, Todoist, Things, Bear?
- **Behavioral moats** — muscle memory, keyboard shortcuts, workflows. What's the actual half-life? Any public retention curves?
- **Network effects in single-player productivity** — Booking pages? Templates marketplaces? Public shared notes? Do any productivity apps have meaningful single-product network effects, or is the "PLG via templates" thesis (Notion, ClickUp) just acquisition-not-retention?
- **Compliance / sovereignty / on-prem** — does selling to enterprises as the path to defensibility actually work for solo / small-team founders, or is it a trap?
- **Personal-data-graph moats** — does owning a user's task history → estimation accuracy → forecasting actually translate to retention, or is it overhyped?
- **Switching costs historically** — what's the half-life of churn at Todoist, Things, Notion, Bear, Obsidian, Logseq? Any public data, founder interviews, or third-party studies?

### 2. Pricing models that survive AI commoditization

Per-user SaaS is under pressure. What pricing models are actually working for productivity apps in 2025-2026?

- Lifetime deals (Setapp, Notion deals, Things) — sustainable or escape hatch?
- Usage-based pricing — anyone in productivity doing this well?
- Hybrid (base + AI-tokens overage) — Notion AI, Sunsama AI, etc.
- Sovereign-data / self-hosted (Obsidian Sync, Logseq, Anytype) — viable revenue?
- White-label / API-first (Cal.com, Linear's API, Todoist's API)
- Free-with-ads (does anyone do this in productivity?)
- Family / team tier dynamics — what converts vs. what doesn't
- Channel models — does B2B2C through bigger platforms work for solo founders?
- Freemium ratios that retain — what % of paid converts at the leading apps? Cite if possible.

## Output format

~1,500-2,000 words. Section headers matching the two numbered sections above. Cite primary sources inline.

End with **a punchy one-paragraph stance** on which "moat" categories are real and which are apocryphal for a solo founder building in this space.
